Benguela

Benguela, in terms of its musical structure, is played in a 6/8 time signature and features a recurring melody. The berimbau, a one-stringed instrument, usually carries the primary melody, while the atabaque, a type of drum, provides the rhythm, and the pandeiro, a tambourine, contributes accents and fills.

Benguela is also associated with specific movements and techniques within Capoeira Angola. For instance, the ginga, which is the fundamental Capoeira movement, is often executed slowly and deliberately during Benguela. Moreover, kicks like meia lua de frente and tesoura are frequently employed within this rhythm.

Regarding its origins, Benguela is believed to have emerged from the Benguela region in Angola, a significant hub for the slave trade during the colonial era. This rhythm is thought to symbolize the struggle and resistance of enslaved Africans who were transported to Brazil and utilized Capoeira as a means of self-defense and rebellion against their oppressors.
